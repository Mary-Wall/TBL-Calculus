Verify that each of the following is an indeterminate form, and use L'Hopital's rule to evaluate the limit.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\lim_{x \rightarrow 2} \frac{x-2}{x^2-4}$
    \item $\lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{2x+7}{3x^2 - 5}$
    \item $\lim{x \rightarrow 0^+} x\ln(x)$. Try rewriting $x$ as $$\frac{1}{1/x}$, and get an indeterminate form.
    \item $\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+}x^x$
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Rewrite this as $\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+}e^{\ln(x^x)}$
        \item Use properties of logarithms to bring the exponent in front of the logarithm.
        \item Use properties of limits to bring the limit into the exponent (since $e^x$ is a continuous function).
        \item The exponent should now be the limit you took above. Evaluate from there.
    \end{itemize}
    \item $\lim_{x \rightarrow 3} \frac{1/x - 1/3}{x^2-9}$
    \item $\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{x^2e^x}{\tan^2(x)}$
    \item $\lim_{x \rightarrow \infty}\frac{3 + \ln(x)}{x^2 + 7}$
    \item $\lim_{x \rightarrow \infty}\ln(x)^{1/x}$
    \item $\lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} (\sqrt{x^2+1} - \sqrt{x +1})$. Try multiplying and dividing by the conjugate and see if you can get an indeterminate form.
\end{enumerate}
